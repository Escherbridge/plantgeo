## Overview

Keyline design is a landscape planning method developed by P.A. Yeomans that uses the natural contours of the land to maximize water retention and soil development. By identifying the keyline — the topographic inflection point between ridgeline and valley floor — practitioners can design irrigation channels, roads, and tillage patterns that spread water evenly across slopes rather than allowing it to concentrate and erode valleys.

The technique involves cutting shallow parallel channels (keylines) slightly off-contour so that water flows outward from valley centers toward ridgelines. This counterintuitive routing charges entire hillsides with moisture, dramatically increasing the effective water-holding capacity of a property and building deep, fertile soil over time.

## Benefits

- Increases on-farm water retention by 40–80% in dry climates
- Reduces erosion by slowing and spreading runoff across slopes
- Builds soil organic matter through even moisture distribution
- Creates drought resilience by recharging subsoil water tables
- Improves pasture productivity without irrigation infrastructure

## Site Requirements

- Minimum 3–10 ha of contiguous land with variable slope (5–30%)
- Moderately permeable soils (loam, clay-loam) that can hold moisture
- Annual rainfall ≥ 300 mm or supplemental irrigation available
- Access for subsoil plow or keyline plow equipment

## Implementation Steps

1. **Survey topography** — identify valley floors, ridgelines, and keypoints on each water course
2. **Design keylines** — run lines parallel to the keypoint contour, sloping 1:800 off-contour toward ridges
3. **Cut keyline channels** — use a Yeomans keyline plow to open subsoil absorption slots along keylines
4. **Install dams or ponds** — place water storage at keypoints to feed gravity-fed irrigation
5. **Establish perennial pasture** — seed deep-rooted grasses along keylines to maintain channels
6. **Annual deep tillage** — run the plow each dry season for 3–5 years to build soil structure

## Expected Outcomes

Within 3–5 years of consistent keyline management, most properties see measurable increases in topsoil depth, reduction in runoff, and improved carrying capacity for livestock or crops. Carbon sequestration rates of 1–3 tC/ha/yr are achievable on previously degraded hillside pasture.
