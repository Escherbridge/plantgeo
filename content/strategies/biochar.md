## Overview

Biochar is a stable, carbon-rich material produced by heating organic matter (biomass) at high temperatures in the absence of oxygen — a process called pyrolysis. When applied to soil, biochar can persist for hundreds to thousands of years, effectively locking atmospheric carbon into the ground. Beyond carbon sequestration, biochar improves soil structure, water-holding capacity, and nutrient retention, particularly in sandy or heavily degraded soils with low organic matter.

Biochar is most beneficial on soils with low soil organic carbon (SOC < 10 g/kg), high bulk density, poor water retention, or acidic pH. It is less effective on already-rich soils and may temporarily reduce plant-available nutrients if applied incorrectly. Feedstocks include agricultural residues, wood chips, manure, and invasive plant biomass — making it a natural fit for wildfire-prone areas producing excess woody debris.

## Benefits

- Sequesters 0.5–2 tC/ha per application with near-permanent stability
- Increases soil water-holding capacity by 10–25% in sandy soils
- Raises soil pH in acid soils, reducing lime requirements
- Improves nutrient use efficiency — reduces fertilizer leaching
- Can utilize wildfire hazard-reduction slash as feedstock

## Site Requirements

- SOC-deficient soils (< 10 g/kg) show strongest response
- Cropland, degraded pasture, or rehabilitated mine land NLCD classes
- Biomass feedstock within 50 km (transport cost limits economics)
- Adequate equipment access for soil incorporation
- Avoid waterlogged or already high-SOC soils (marginal benefit)

## Implementation Steps

1. **Source feedstock** — identify local biomass: wood chips from fire fuels reduction, agricultural residues, invasive species removal debris
2. **Produce biochar** — use a retort or flame-curtain kiln (Kon-Tiki) targeting 450–700°C; aim for ≥ 70% fixed carbon content
3. **Charge biochar** — soak or mix biochar with compost, urine, or liquid fertilizer for 2–4 weeks to pre-load nutrients before application
4. **Determine rate** — start at 5–20 t/ha; avoid exceeding 30 t/ha in a single application
5. **Incorporate** — till biochar into top 15–25 cm of soil; surface application is less effective but acceptable for no-till systems
6. **Monitor and evaluate** — sample soil SOC and bulk density at years 1, 3, and 5 to document improvement

## Expected Outcomes

Biochar amendments on low-SOC soils typically show measurable improvement in water retention and crop yields within 1–3 growing seasons. Carbon sequestration is essentially permanent on human timescales. Economic returns are strongest when combined with on-farm pyrolysis using fire-hazard woody biomass as feedstock.
