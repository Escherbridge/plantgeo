## Overview

Silvopasture is an agroforestry practice that intentionally integrates trees with livestock grazing systems on the same land unit. Unlike conventional pasture or plantation forestry, silvopasture manages trees, forages, and animals together to create a more productive and ecologically resilient system. Trees provide shade, fodder, timber, and carbon storage, while livestock graze beneath and between tree rows, fertilizing the soil and controlling understory vegetation.

Silvopasture has been practiced for centuries in Europe, Latin America, and parts of Africa under names such as dehesa (Spain/Portugal) and bocage (France). Modern implementations use alley cropping arrangements, widely spaced hardwood rows, or riparian buffer plantings to balance light availability for pasture grasses with rapid tree establishment.

## Benefits

- Sequesters 2–10 tCO₂/ha/yr depending on tree density and species
- Provides livestock shade and shelter, reducing heat stress and improving animal welfare
- Diversifies farm income with timber, nuts, fruit, or fodder crops
- Improves soil organic matter through leaf litter and deep root systems
- Reduces input costs through reduced fertilizer needs and natural pest control

## Site Requirements

- Moderate drainage class (well-drained to somewhat poorly drained)
- Soil organic carbon ≥ 5 g/kg — low SOC indicates need for soil building first
- Annual precipitation ≥ 500 mm or reliable supplemental irrigation
- Slope < 25% to allow machinery access between tree rows
- Existing pasture or degraded cropland with grazing potential

## Implementation Steps

1. **Select tree species** — choose multi-purpose species suited to local climate (e.g., black walnut, persimmon, honey locust, pear, oak)
2. **Design tree rows** — orient rows east–west for maximum light penetration; space trees 10–15 m apart within rows, rows 15–25 m apart
3. **Establish trees** — plant bare-root stock in early spring; protect from livestock with wire cages or temporary fencing
4. **Manage exclusion zones** — exclude livestock from newly planted rows for 2–4 years until trees are established
5. **Introduce rotational grazing** — graze beneath trees in short high-intensity rotations to stimulate grass and control competition
6. **Harvest and prune** — begin timber pruning at year 5–10 to develop straight boles and allow light to pasture

## Expected Outcomes

Mature silvopasture systems (15–30 years) show 30–50% increases in total farm productivity per hectare compared to either monoculture pasture or plantation forestry alone. Carbon stocks accumulate in both soil and woody biomass. Livestock performance typically improves by 5–15% due to shade and fodder supplementation.
