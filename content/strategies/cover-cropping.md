## Overview

Cover cropping is the practice of growing crops specifically to benefit the soil rather than for harvest. Cover crops — including legumes, grasses, brassicas, and mixed blends — are grown during fallow periods or interseeded into cash crops to protect and enrich the soil. They prevent erosion by keeping the soil surface covered, suppress weeds, fix atmospheric nitrogen (legumes), and feed the soil microbiome through root exudates and decomposing biomass.

Cover crops are particularly valuable on cropland experiencing declining soil organic matter, compaction, or erosion risk — common conditions in irrigated annual crop systems of the western USA. When managed as living mulches or terminated before seeding, they create the soil biology and structure that allows reduction or elimination of synthetic fertilizers over a 3–10 year transition.

## Benefits

- Builds soil organic matter by 0.1–0.5% per year with consistent use
- Fixes 50–200 kg N/ha/yr with legume-dominated mixes (reduces fertilizer cost)
- Reduces erosion by 50–90% compared to bare fallow
- Suppresses weed pressure, reducing herbicide use
- Improves water infiltration and reduces surface crusting

## Site Requirements

- Cropland or degraded agricultural land (NLCD classes 81–82)
- Sufficient soil moisture or irrigation access for cover crop establishment
- Appropriate termination window before cash crop planting (45–90 days)
- Soil texture: works best on loam, sandy loam, clay loam
- Equipment access for seeding and termination (roller-crimper or mow-and-blow)

## Implementation Steps

1. **Select species mix** — match to season and goals: winter rye + hairy vetch for nitrogen; radish + oat for compaction; clover for long-term fertility
2. **Seed into stubble or fallow** — broadcast or drill seed within 2 weeks of cash crop harvest; aerial seeding into standing corn or soy is viable
3. **Manage for biomass** — allow 60–90 days of growth before termination to maximize biomass and root development
4. **Terminate before seeding** — roll-crimp or mow at flowering stage; avoid incorporation if no-till system
5. **Evaluate soil health** — take Haney test or PLFA analysis after 3 years to measure biological activity improvement
6. **Adjust mix annually** — rotate cover crop families to prevent disease carryover and broaden soil biology benefits

## Expected Outcomes

Cover cropping shows measurable soil improvements within 3–5 years on most cropland. Nitrogen credits from legume termination typically offset 30–60% of synthetic N requirements. Long-term practitioners (10+ years) frequently report elimination of starter fertilizer and reduction in pest pressure. Carbon sequestration of 0.3–1.0 tC/ha/yr is well-documented in peer-reviewed literature.
