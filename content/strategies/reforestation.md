## Overview

Reforestation is the process of replanting or naturally regenerating tree cover on land where forests have been degraded or removed. It is one of the highest-impact interventions for climate mitigation, biodiversity restoration, and watershed protection. On degraded lands in fire-prone or drought-affected regions, reforestation must account for local fire regimes, appropriate species selection, and the hydrological capacity of the site to support trees long-term.

Modern reforestation practice distinguishes between passive restoration (removing stressors and allowing natural regeneration), assisted natural regeneration (protecting and managing volunteer seedlings), and active planting. The most cost-effective approach depends on seed availability, soil condition, and land-use history.

## Benefits

- Sequesters 3–20 tCO₂/ha/yr depending on species, rainfall, and site quality
- Restores watershed function and reduces peak flows
- Creates habitat corridors for wildlife migration
- Reduces surface temperatures and modifies local climate
- Provides long-term timber, biomass, and non-timber forest products

## Site Requirements

- Degraded NLCD land cover class (shrubland 52, grassland 71, or pasture 81)
- Annual precipitation ≥ 400 mm or access to supplemental water in first 3 years
- Fire risk must be managed — high-severity fire zones require fire-adapted species
- Soil pH 5.5–7.5, sufficient depth (≥ 30 cm to restrictive layer)
- Low development pressure and secure land tenure

## Implementation Steps

1. **Site assessment** — sample soils, identify existing vegetation, assess seed bank potential and fire history
2. **Select species** — prioritize native, drought-adapted, fire-resilient species for western sites (e.g., ponderosa pine, blue oak, canyon live oak, manzanita, ceanothus)
3. **Site preparation** — remove invasive grasses or shrubs competing with seedlings; avoid full cultivation on erosion-prone slopes
4. **Plant or seed** — plant container stock in late fall before rains; direct seed annuals for nurse crop competition
5. **Protect seedlings** — install deer guards and mulch rings; manage gophers; supplement water in year 1–2 drought periods
6. **Fire management** — thin ladder fuels at year 5–10; maintain low-fuel spacing around young trees

## Expected Outcomes

Successful reforestation on suitable sites in western USA achieves 70–90% survival rates in years 1–3 with adequate moisture. Canopy closure typically occurs at 15–25 years for conifers, 10–15 years for fast-growing oaks. Soil carbon and watershed benefits accumulate over decades.
