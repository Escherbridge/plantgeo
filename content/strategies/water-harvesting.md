## Overview

Water harvesting encompasses a range of passive techniques that capture, slow, and infiltrate rainfall and runoff before it leaves a landscape. Swales — level-bottomed trenches dug on contour — are the most widely used water-harvesting earthwork. Water harvesting also includes berms, check dams, retention basins, rain gardens, and catchment tanks. Together these techniques transform degraded, drought-prone land by rebuilding its capacity to absorb and retain precipitation.

In arid and semi-arid regions experiencing increasing drought stress, water harvesting is often the foundational intervention that makes other restoration practices viable. By recharging the soil moisture profile during brief rain events, swales enable plant establishment in areas that would otherwise fail to support vegetation.

## Benefits

- Captures and infiltrates 50–90% of runoff from design storm events
- Recharges shallow groundwater and spring flows
- Reduces erosion and downstream flood peaks
- Creates moist planting niches for trees and perennials in dry climates
- Very low cost per hectare compared to irrigation infrastructure

## Site Requirements

- Slope 1–20% — too flat reduces effectiveness, too steep creates failure risk
- Permeable soils (sandy loam to loam) capable of absorbing water
- Precipitation deficit (annual PET exceeds annual rainfall)
- Sufficient contributing catchment area to fill swales during rain events
- No subsurface hardpan within 60 cm of surface (impedes infiltration)

## Implementation Steps

1. **Survey on-contour lines** — use a bunyip level, A-frame, or laser level to mark true contour lines across the slope
2. **Design swale layout** — space swales based on slope and rainfall: steeper slopes need closer spacing (10–20 m); gentle slopes 25–50 m
3. **Excavate swales** — dig flat-bottomed trenches 30–60 cm deep; pile spoil on downhill side to form a berm
4. **Install overflow spillways** — compact rock or concrete spillways at each swale end to safely pass excess water
5. **Plant swale berms** — immediately seed or plant berms with deep-rooted perennials, shrubs, or trees to stabilize earthworks
6. **Monitor first rain events** — inspect for erosion, overflow, or under-infiltration; adjust spillway heights as needed

## Expected Outcomes

Water harvesting earthworks become more effective over time as vegetation establishes on berms and soil biology improves infiltration. In arid regions receiving 250–500 mm/yr, well-designed swale systems can increase effective soil moisture by the equivalent of 100–200 mm/yr additional rainfall. Groundwater recharge effects are measurable within 2–5 years in responsive aquifers.
